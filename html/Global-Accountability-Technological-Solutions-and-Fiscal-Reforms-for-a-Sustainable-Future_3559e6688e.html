Topics
Military spending limits
Countries in the developed world should not be allowed to spend more than 0.5% of GDP on the military. The spending on environmental conservation efforts must always be at least 2.5% of GDP. This will allow developing nations to not be bullied by wealthier nations that spend heavily on their militaries.
Population caps
All nations should have a population limit beyond which those nations will pay higher tariffs or have other forms of economic sanctions levied on them. This will help mitigate runaway population explosions in the world and incentivise nations and/or communities to prioritise effective family planning.
Tax on billionaires
Taxation laws should be modified such that hoarding of money in individual entities is not possible. Thus no billionaires should be allowed and any excess income over a certain limit should be appropriated by the state and redistributed to others in the population. This will de-incentivise brutalist practices within business.
Technology investment
Technological solutions should be sought for environmental issues. This will ensure that current rates of growth in the economy can be sustained without harm to the environment.
Wealthy Nation Obligation
Nations with more wealth, especially nations from the 'global north' which were involved in colonial practices, should bear a higher cost burden for combatting climate change. They have an obligation to pay for the damages caused by the historical exploitative practices and the current extractive policies that have led to their higher wealth status.

Instructor Report

This assignment asked students to debate a series of bold policy proposals focused on balancing economic growth with environmental sustainability. The discussions tackled proposals ranging from technological investments and wealth redistribution to military spending limits and population caps, with Guide pushing the students to dig deeper into underlying assumptions and trade-offs.

Themes

  • Debates on wealth redistribution emerged strongly. Many discussions centered on whether strict wealth caps or high taxation could correct systemic imbalances without stifling innovation.
  • Government intervention was a recurring focus. Students weighed the merits of proactive regulation against the risks of overreach and inefficiency.
  • Trade-offs between defensive spending and environmental investment appeared repeatedly. Multiple groups examined how reallocating funds from military budgets could support sustainability, though with differing opinions on feasibility.
  • Ethical and practical issues related to population controls were frequently challenged. The debates highlighted concerns about fairness and enforcement, revealing the complexity behind setting such limits.

Common Ground

  • Students agreed on the need for balanced and nuanced policies. Despite divergent viewpoints, many recognized that neither extreme market freedom nor heavy-handed government control offered complete solutions.
  • There was consensus on the value of questioning assumptions. Across groups, students appreciated Guides approach in urging them to investigate the real-world costs and benefits of each proposal.

Persistent Disagreements

  • Wealth redistribution strategies sparked deep divisions. Some argued that strict wealth caps would prevent exploitation, while others felt such measures risked damning economic consequences.
  • Disagreement persisted on capping military spending. One camp favored redirecting funds toward environmental projects, whereas the other underscored the indispensability of national security, leaving the conflict largely unresolved.

Guide's Role

  • Guide consistently challenged students assumptions with pointed questions. Its interventions forced the group to justify claims and dive into the details of their arguments.
  • Guide helped maintain focus and drive deeper analysis. By questioning both broad premises and nuanced points, Guide encouraged the students to refine their views and engage critically with contested ideas.

Insights

  • The discussions revealed unexpected depth and nuance. Students moved beyond surface arguments to explore how ethical, economic, and practical factors intertwine in real-world policy-making.
  • Real-world examples enriched the debate. When students linked theoretical proposals to tangible challenges, the conversations gained practical significance and clarity.

Lessons

  • Challenging prompts by Guide drove richer conversations. Future sessions may benefit from similarly direct questioning to help debunk oversimplified arguments.
  • Encouraging students to confront contradictory claims proved effective. Instructors might build on this approach by addressing recurring misconceptions and exploring balanced alternatives in follow-up discussions.
