Instructor Report

The assignment asked students to examine and debate four major ethical frameworks regarding abortion: Singer’s view on low moral consideration of the fetus, Thomson’s emphasis on women’s right to bodily autonomy, Marquis’s argument that a fetus’s future makes abortion immoral, and Hendricks’s assertion that abortion is wrong even if the fetus lacks personhood. The discussions unfolded across multiple groups with the Sway facilitator Guide prompting students to clarify and deepen their analyses while encouraging them to consider nuanced ethical, legal, and contextual factors.

Themes

  • Common ground was found around the need to balance fetal potential with women’s autonomy. Many participants agreed that, despite deep-rooted differences, both perspectives recognize exceptional circumstances—such as cases of coercion or extreme vulnerability—that complicate straightforward ethical judgments.
  • Evolving perspectives emerged as participants refined their arguments over time. Initial rigid stances softened in several groups, with one participant noting how “the fetus’s potential” should be weighed against lived realities, prompting a more nuanced reassessment of moral responsibility.
  • Persistent disagreement centered on criteria for moral status and when rights might conflict. Multiple discussions revealed enduring divides over whether inherent biological traits or future potential should drive moral conclusions, and debates often circled back to the ambiguous threshold for personhood.
  • Guide’s role was instrumental in driving deeper inquiry and holding participants accountable. Guide consistently interjected with incisive questions—challenging assumptions and calling out overgeneralizations—to help reveal inconsistencies in reasoning and encourage precise definitions of contentious terms.

Insights

  • Student reasoning deepened as discussions progressed. Many groups moved from clear-cut positions to reflecting on the complexities of ethical frameworks after sustained dialogue and targeted questions from Guide.
  • Real-world contexts enriched the debates significantly. Participants frequently invoked socioeconomic, medical, and cultural factors to illustrate how abstract ethical theories play out in practical settings, thereby broadening the scope of the discussion.

Lessons

  • Sway’s structured dialogue effectively promotes critical thinking and self-correction. The iterative nature of the discussions, along with Guide’s probing inquiries, encouraged students to re-examine their assumptions and engage with multiple ethical dimensions simultaneously.
  • Instructors might consider follow-up sessions focused on clarifying the contested concepts of personhood and bodily autonomy. Addressing these persistent ethical dilemmas directly could further help students reconcile theoretical ideals with the complex realities underscored in their debates.
