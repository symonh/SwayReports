Instructor Report

This assignment asked students to debate three contentious topics: whether amputation is an appropriate treatment for apotemnophilia, if medical paternalism can be justified by potential patient benefits, and whether misleading or nudging patients violates their right to autonomy. Discussions held on Sway—facilitated by Guide—challenged students to balance ethical principles, evaluate clinical guidelines, and consider the broader societal and financial implications of healthcare decisions.

Themes

  • Common ground emerged around the need for rigorous evaluation and ethical safeguards. Students across multiple groups agreed on the importance of comprehensive psychological assessments, transparent communication, and clear consent processes as fundamental to responsible medical decision-making.
  • Persistent disagreements centered on the boundaries of patient autonomy versus medical paternalism. Divergent views appeared regarding when it is ethically acceptable to override full disclosure—especially in emergency or high-risk scenarios—and whether irreversible procedures should ever be justified, with students grappling with the nuances of these tensions.
  • Guide consistently pushed students to refine their reasoning and question assumptions. Its incisive questions, such as asking, "What ethical limits justify nudging or withholding information?" forced participants to explore hidden dimensions, balance competing values, and ultimately reach a more nuanced understanding of complex issues.

Insights

  • Discussions evolved from rigid stances toward more nuanced positions over time. Iterative challenges by Guide encouraged participants to rethink initial assumptions, leading to deeper explorations of how cultural values, insurance constraints, and healthcare equity impact decision-making.
  • The integration of theoretical ethics with practical concerns enriched the debate. Students linked abstract ethical frameworks to real-world issues—such as the potential for financial burdens or discrimination—which offers instructors valuable insights into how ethical theory translates into everyday clinical practice.

Lessons

  • The structured format proved effective in fostering critical ethical reasoning. The blend of peer dialogue and Guide’s probing questions enabled students to articulate and refine complex positions, suggesting that future discussions should continue emphasizing reflective debate over simple dichotomies.
  • Encouraging the examination of multifaceted dilemmas can enhance understanding of clinical ethics. Guide’s role in challenging students to consider variables like cultural biases, public health risks, and individual patient needs highlights a promising approach for developing nuanced perspectives in medical education.
