Instructor Report

The assignment asked students to debate three contentious endoflife issues: whether it is morally permissible to grant a euthanasia request in cases like that of Aurelia Brouwers, whether active euthanasia should be legalized globally, and whether killing is morally equivalent to letting die. In these discussions, pairs (and sometimes trios) of college students grappled with patient autonomy, the assessment of mental versus physical suffering, and the ethics of active versus passive decisions regarding life and deathall under the persistent and probing questioning of Guide.

Themes

  • Students found common ground on valuing patient autonomy. Many participants agreed that respecting a patients choice is essential when addressing euthanasia, even as they debated the limits and safeguards needed to prevent abuse.

  • Participants acknowledged the importance of establishing clear criteria. Despite differing opinions on whether treatment-resistant mental suffering equals terminal physical illness, there was mutual recognition of the need for objective standards in justifying euthanasia.

  • Disagreements were persistent on the differences between mental and physical suffering. Debates centered around whether mental suffering should be treated with the same ethical urgency as physical suffering and how to measure treatment resistance and quality of life.

  • Contention also surfaced over the moral distinction between killing and letting die. Students struggled to reconcile intent with outcome, leading to unresolved tensions that Guide repeatedly challenged through hypotheticals and scenario-based inquiries.

  • Guide consistently pushed for deeper scrutiny of assumptions. Its targeted questions prompted students to justify their positions and consider nuances, such as whether an act of omission might be as morally culpable as a direct action.

  • Guides interventions sharpened the debate by highlighting potential biases and oversights. By asking for concrete examples and clearer criteria, Guide helped students refine their arguments and recognize the complexity behind seemingly straightforward ethical claims.

Insights

  • Students frequently employed concrete examples to challenge abstract ethical claims. Their use of real-world scenarios, like contrasting treatment by a doctor in terminal and psychiatric cases, enriched the discussion and revealed practical challenges in applying ethical principles.
  • The debates showcased an eagerness to blend personal convictions with a broader analysis of social implications. Participants were not only concerned with individual rights but also with the potential long-term societal impacts of legalizing active euthanasia and reframing life-and-death decisions.

Possible Misconceptions

  • Some discussions blurred the line between clinical uncertainty and irreversible conditions. A few groups appeared to conflate treatment-resistant mental illness with conditions that are objectively terminal, an error that Guide worked hard to correct through probing questions.
  • There was occasional reliance on intuitive judgments about quality of life. This tendency risked oversimplifying complex medical and ethical factors, leading to assumptions that required further clarification and evidence during the exchanges.

Lessons

  • The need for clear, objective criteria in ethical decisions became evident. Professors may wish to follow up with the class on how to construct precise guidelines that differentiate between various types of suffering and validate or challenge claims about quality of life.
  • Guides method of challenging students to justify their claims deepened the overall debate. This suggests that incorporating targeted prompts and hypotheticals can be highly effective in sharpening reasoning and fostering more nuanced discussions on controversial topics.
