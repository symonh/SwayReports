Instructor Report

This assignment asked students to engage with difficult ethical dilemmas in healthcare resource allocation—from deciding liver transplant eligibility for patients battling alcoholism to weighing the impact of market-based distribution and disability discrimination. Across multiple Sway chats, students debated both theoretical principles and pragmatic challenges, while Guide consistently pushed them to refine their reasoning and explore the nuances behind each position.

Themes

  • Students found common ground in upholding fairness and non-discrimination as core values in healthcare. They generally agreed that ethical resource allocation should balance individual needs with systemic equity, despite differing views on specific criteria.
  • Debate often centered on persistent disagreements over allocation criteria. Students remained divided on whether to prioritize strict equality, personal responsibility, or utilitarian outcomes when scarce resources were at stake, and these differences sometimes deepened even after Guide’s probing questions.
  • Guide played a pivotal role in deepening the ethical inquiry. By challenging simplistic assumptions—asking incisive questions about the fairness of objective metrics versus individualized care—Guide helped students explore the complexities underlying each debate, even when discussions occasionally veered off-topic.

Insights

  • Student discussions revealed a strong willingness to grapple with multifaceted ethical dilemmas. Although initial positions were often polarized, the structured dialogue led many to appreciate the importance of nuanced policy-making in healthcare resource allocation.
  • The conversations effectively bridged theoretical ethics with real-world challenges. Several groups connected abstract ethical principles with practical issues like medical urgency and systemic inequities, providing robust insights that could inform future policy discussions.

Misconceptions

  • Some students oversimplified market-driven healthcare by assuming that the ability to pay directly justified treatment eligibility. This view often overlooked the broader ethical mandate of equitable access, sparking friction when contrasted with more inclusive perspectives.
  • There was a recurring misconception that strict equality necessarily provides a fair solution. In debates on liver transplantation and disability discrimination, students struggled to reconcile the notion of identical treatment with the need for context-sensitive decisions.

Lessons

  • Guide’s incisive questioning consistently deepened the quality of discussion and prompted critical self-reflection. Instructors might consider highlighting these moments in follow-up sessions to reinforce the value of probing inquiry in ethical debates.
  • Balancing open-ended exploration with structured debate emerged as key to productive dialogue. Future Sway chats could benefit from clearer frameworks or case studies to keep discussions focused while still encouraging rich, reflective exploration.
