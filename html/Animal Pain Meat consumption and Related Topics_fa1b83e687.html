Instructor Report

This assignment asked students to critically examine ethical dilemmas involving animal pain, the consumption of factory-farmed meat, and the permissibility of medical experiments on animals versus cognitively similar humans. Throughout their paired discussions, students grappled with longstanding debates about species membership, cognitive capacities, and the proper metrics for evaluating and prioritizing suffering, while Guide consistently pushed them to question assumptions and articulate deeper ethical reasoning.

Themes

  • Common ground emerged around the recognition that all forms of suffering warrant ethical consideration. Many groups agreed that both animal and human pain deserve attention even though they varied in their judgments on how to balance these concerns.
  • Persistent disagreement centered on whether cognitive abilities and species membership justify differing moral weight. Several discussions remained divided about if and how human cognitive complexity and cultural legacy should trump the intrinsic suffering experienced by animals.
  • Guide’s role was pivotal in deepening the ethical debate through incisive, challenging questions. Guide repeatedly prompted students to revisit and clarify their positions, using thought experiments and targeted interventions to highlight inconsistencies in their reasoning.

Insights

  • Repeated dialogue patterns indicate a strong influence from Guide’s scripted interventions. Several groups featured nearly identical questions and challenges from Guide, suggesting that the AI’s consistent prompts played a major role in shaping the discussions.
  • Students demonstrated notable depth when interrogating their own assumptions. Many participants moved beyond surface-level arguments by rigorously questioning how to objectively measure pain and considering the broader ethical consequences of species-based distinctions.
