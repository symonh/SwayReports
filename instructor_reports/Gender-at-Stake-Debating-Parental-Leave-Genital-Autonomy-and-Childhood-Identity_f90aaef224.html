Instructor Report

The assigned discussions explored three main topics: (1) whether courts should default to maternal custody in opposite-sex divorces, (2) whether we should respect female genital mutilation (FGM) as a cultural practice, and (3) whether a gender-neutral approach to genital cutting requires treating male circumcision and FGM equally. Students engaged in structured dialogues facilitated by Guide, Sway's AI discussion facilitator designed to help students examine assumptions and deepen their analysis. Across all discussions, students wrestled with tensions between individual rights and cultural traditions, the significance of consent and bodily autonomy, and how to implement ethical principles in practical contexts.

Themes

  • Students frequently began with polarized positions but evolved toward more nuanced stances through dialogue. Initial exchanges often featured stark contrasts—tradition versus human rights, maternal instinct versus gender equality—before developing into more complex analyses that acknowledged competing values and contextual factors.

  • Consent emerged as a central ethical principle across topics. Whether discussing custody arrangements, circumcision, or FGM, students repeatedly emphasized informed, voluntary choice as a fundamental consideration. Many concluded that procedures performed on children unable to consent demand especially strong justification.

  • Students consistently sought to balance respect for cultural traditions with protection from harm. Rather than simply imposing universal standards, many advocated for education, community dialogue, and culturally sensitive approaches that could foster internal change while preventing immediate harms.

Guide's role

  • Guide consistently challenged assumptions by asking targeted follow-up questions that exposed contradictions or unexamined premises. When students made broad generalizations about gender roles in parenting or the universality of cultural practices, Guide prompted them to consider exceptions, evidence, and counterarguments.

  • Guide steered conversations from abstract principles toward practical applications. Through questions about implementation challenges, unintended consequences, and specific criteria, Guide helped students clarify their ideas by considering how their ethical frameworks would function in real-world contexts.

  • Guide maintained neutrality while deepening analysis, allowing students to reach their own conclusions. Rather than advocating particular positions, Guide focused on helping students clarify their reasoning, consider multiple perspectives, and reconcile competing values. This approach enabled genuine evolution in student thinking.

Common ground

  • Students widely agreed that individualized assessment is preferable to blanket policies. In custody discussions, most concluded that case-by-case evaluation based on parental fitness rather than gender stereotypes best serves children's interests. Similarly, when discussing cultural practices, many advocated for contextual approaches rather than universal prohibitions.

  • Students converged on education and community involvement as key pathways to ethical change. Across topics, participants emphasized that meaningful transformation requires engaging stakeholders, providing balanced information, and creating safe spaces for dialogue rather than simply imposing external values or regulations.

  • Most discussions arrived at the principle that protecting vulnerable individuals from harm should take precedence over abstract cultural or religious claims. While respecting diverse traditions, students generally agreed that practices causing significant, non-consensual harm—particularly to children—cannot be justified solely through appeals to culture.

Persistent disagreements

  • Students diverged on whether medical benefits can justify non-consensual procedures. Some argued that potential health advantages of male circumcision distinguish it from FGM, while others maintained that bodily autonomy trumps these considerations absent immediate medical necessity.

  • Opinions varied on the proper role of external intervention in cultural practices. Some students favored a hands-off approach that allows communities to evolve internally, while others argued that certain universal human rights justify limited outside pressure or support.

  • Discussions revealed ongoing tension between parents' decision-making authority and children's future autonomy. Students struggled to reconcile respecting parental rights with protecting children's long-term interests, particularly when permanent physical alterations were involved.

Insights

  • Students demonstrated considerable intellectual maturity in navigating competing ethical frameworks. Rather than simply asserting personal opinions, many explicitly acknowledged the limitations of their cultural perspectives and the complexity of balancing universal principles with contextual sensitivity.

  • The most productive discussions occurred when students moved beyond binary thinking to explore unexpected middle grounds. Creative solutions emerged when participants considered alternative rituals, delayed procedures until the age of consent, or proposed educational interventions that preserved cultural meaning while preventing harm.

  • Personal experiences significantly influenced students' perspectives, sometimes prompting dramatic shifts in position. Several discussions showed how firsthand accounts or family histories regarding custody arrangements, religious practices, or cultural traditions could transform abstract debates into deeply felt ethical reflections.

Possible misconceptions

  • Some discussions suggested confusion about the medical evidence regarding male circumcision. Students sometimes made confident assertions about health benefits or risks without distinguishing between stronger evidence (e.g., reduced STI transmission in high-prevalence settings) and more contested claims.

  • A few students initially equated opposition to mandatory practices with disrespect for the cultures involved. This framing sometimes confused critiquing a practice with dismissing an entire cultural tradition, though most students eventually distinguished between respecting a culture and endorsing all its components.

Lessons

  • The FGM topic provided particularly rich ground for exploring the tension between cultural sensitivity and universal rights. Students engaged deeply with this complex issue, moving beyond simplistic condemnation or relativism to consider gradual, community-based approaches to harmful practices.

  • Hypothetical policy scenarios prompted especially concrete analysis of ethical principles. When Guide asked students to propose specific guidelines, age thresholds, or implementation strategies, discussions became more nuanced and revealed the practical challenges of applying abstract values.
