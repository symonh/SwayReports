Instructor Report

This assignment encouraged students to analyze ethical questions surrounding beauty standards, media representation, income inequality, and aging. Students engaged in substantive discussions about societal pressures, personal autonomy, and systemic change. Many discussions demonstrated students' ability to move beyond initial polarized positions toward more nuanced understanding while considering practical solutions.

Themes

  • Most students evolved from binary positions to more nuanced perspectives. In discussions about cosmetic procedures, students often began with opposing viewpoints—defending either bodily autonomy or criticizing beauty standards—before finding middle ground recognizing both individual choice and systemic pressures. One student initially argued, "Criticizing women for getting Botox or plastic surgery is unnecessary and harmful" but later acknowledged that "the beauty industry deserves critique, not the women who use these services."

  • Conversations frequently revealed tensions between personal choice and societal responsibility. Whether discussing cosmetic procedures, age-gap relationships in films, or economic inequality, students wrestled with balancing respect for individual agency against broader social impacts. This was particularly evident in debates about media representation, where students questioned how cultural products shape audience perceptions while respecting creative freedom.

  • Students consistently advocated for educational approaches and awareness campaigns as solutions. Across topics, students proposed targeted interventions like media literacy programs, classroom discussions about income inequality, and awareness campaigns about erectile dysfunction. This preference for educational strategies often bridged initially divergent positions, providing common ground for students with different starting perspectives.

Guide's role

  • Guide consistently challenged students' assumptions and pushed for evidence-based reasoning. When students made claims about cosmetic procedures perpetuating unrealistic beauty standards or age-gap films normalizing power imbalances, Guide pressed for specifics: "Could you elaborate on what you mean by 'warped social standpoint'?" and "Are there any specific scenarios or character dynamics that might make a larger age gap more or less acceptable?"

  • Guide skillfully steered discussions from abstract opinions toward concrete examples and actionable solutions. By asking pointed questions about specific films, media campaigns, or policy interventions, Guide helped move conversations from theoretical disagreement to practical proposals. In discussions about income inequality, Guide prompted students to consider "how improving access to vocational training or apprenticeships might impact both income inequality and the gender pay gap."

  • Guide maintained an intellectually demanding stance that pushed students to consider multiple perspectives. Rather than validating one position, Guide consistently asked students to engage with their partner's concerns and to consider alternate viewpoints. This approach was particularly effective when discussions touched on sensitive topics like gender dynamics or wealth distribution, helping students develop more comprehensive analyses.

Common ground

  • Students generally agreed that context matters when evaluating ethical questions. In discussions about age gaps in films, most students rejected blanket boycotts in favor of more nuanced criteria based on how relationships are portrayed. As one student noted, "Not every age gap is inherently problematic but deserves scrutiny when it feels exploitative."

  • Most students found common ground in advocating for transparency and honest representation. Whether discussing beauty standards, income reporting, or media portrayals, students agreed that transparency serves both individual autonomy and social awareness. This perspective unified discussions about cosmetic procedures, with students agreeing that influencers should openly acknowledge their enhancements rather than promoting unattainable "natural" beauty.

  • Students typically converged on multi-faceted approaches combining individual and systemic change. Even when beginning from opposing positions about whether to prioritize the gender pay gap or broader income inequality, students often concluded that both required attention through complementary strategies addressing specific injustices while building broader economic reforms.

Persistent disagreements

  • Students showed consistent disagreement about the effectiveness of boycotts versus other forms of activism. In discussions about age-gap films, some students defended boycotts as concrete economic pressure, while others argued for education, social media campaigns, and industry dialogue. These disagreements revealed different assumptions about how cultural change occurs and what levers most effectively influence industry practices.

  • Debates about whether to frame erectile dysfunction as normal aging or a medical pathology revealed tensions between destigmatization and medical support. Some students worried that overemphasizing ED as "normal" might discourage men from seeking needed treatment, while others argued that reframing could reduce shame and broaden perspectives on intimacy beyond penetrative sex.

  • Discussions about cosmetic procedures revealed persistent tensions between celebrating bodily autonomy and criticizing harmful beauty standards. While students often found theoretical common ground in criticizing industries rather than individuals, practical application of this principle remained challenging, particularly when discussing the role of celebrities and influencers whose personal choices shape broader norms.

Insights

  • Students demonstrated their ability to connect personal experiences with systemic analysis. Several discussions referenced personal examples—from a student's mother's positive experience with cosmetic surgery to observations about workplace barriers faced by women—while linking these experiences to broader societal patterns, showing sophisticated integration of anecdotal and structural thinking.

  • Cross-cultural comparisons enriched several discussions. References to K-pop industry beauty standards, Chinese social media influencers, and varying international approaches to income inequality allowed students to contextualize American norms within global perspectives, enhancing their analytical depth.

Lessons

  • The erectile dysfunction topic generated surprisingly rich discussions about gender, aging, and sexuality. What might have seemed a narrow medical topic expanded into thoughtful analyses of masculinity norms, media representation of aging, and inclusive sexual health education, demonstrating how apparently specific prompts can open into broader social questions.

  • The most productive discussions occurred when students moved beyond binary ethical positions to consider practical implementation challenges. Conversations that began with abstract principles but evolved to address specific contexts, stakeholders, and potential unintended consequences showed the greatest analytical development and yielded the most creative solutions.

  • Discussions about boycotting films with age-gap relationships occasionally conflated fictional portrayals with endorsement. Some students seemed to assume that any depiction of potentially problematic relationships necessarily normalizes them, though this perspective potentially overlooks how critical or complex portrayals might actually challenge such norms.
