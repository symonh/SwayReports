Instructor Report

Students debated philosophical arguments about abortion ethics, focusing particularly on personhood, bodily autonomy, and the moral status of fetuses. They discussed perspectives from Marquis's "future like ours" argument, Thomson's bodily autonomy framework, and other philosophical positions while exploring the tensions between maternal rights and fetal moral status...

Themes

  • Competing frameworks for moral consideration dominated discussions. Students frequently debated whether potential future life (Marquis), viability outside the womb, or present consciousness should determine moral status, with some arguing that "potential isn't enough" while others maintained that development toward personhood creates meaningful moral claims.
  • Students consistently tested moral principles through analogies. Comparisons ranged from violinists and organ donation to home intruders, garden seeds, and wartime dilemmas, revealing how students attempted to apply abstract ethical principles to concrete scenarios that might clarify intuitions about bodily rights versus responsibility toward dependent life.

Guide's Role

  • Guide consistently challenged students to clarify key terms and identify inconsistencies in their positions. When students used ambiguous language about "personhood," "individual," or "child," Guide pressed them to define these terms and confront cases where their principles seemed to conflict, particularly regarding exceptions for rape or maternal health.
  • Through targeted questioning, Guide prevented surface-level agreement and pushed students toward deeper analysis. Guide frequently directed students to examine tensions between their stated principles and intuitions about edge cases, fostering more sophisticated engagement with philosophical arguments rather than allowing debate to remain at the level of slogans or talking points.

Common Ground

  • Students generally agreed that pregnancy represents a unique moral situation requiring careful ethical consideration. Even those with opposing views acknowledged that abortion decisions involve weighing competing moral values rather than simple black-and-white judgments.
  • Many students converged around the idea that late-term abortions raise different moral questions than early-term procedures. While disagreeing about where to draw lines, students frequently recognized developmental milestones as relevant to moral deliberation, suggesting common ground for potential policy compromises.

Persistent Disagreements

  • The question of when moral personhood begins remained fundamentally contested. Some students maintained that conception marks the beginning of a morally significant human life, while others argued that various developmental milestones (heartbeat, viability, consciousness) must be reached before full moral consideration applies.
  • Students could not reconcile bodily autonomy with responsibility toward potential life. While some insisted that a woman's right to bodily self-determination remains paramount throughout pregnancy, others argued that this right becomes constrained by obligations to the developing fetus, particularly as pregnancy progresses.

Insights

  • Personal experiences enriched theoretical discussions in unexpected ways. References to military service, labor and delivery experiences, and family backgrounds allowed students to connect abstract philosophical arguments to lived realities, demonstrating how personal context shapes ethical reasoning.
  • The question of male participation in abortion discourse revealed deeper tensions about representation and experience. Some discussions highlighted disagreement about whether those who cannot personally experience pregnancy should have equal voice in policy decisions, raising important questions about the relationship between lived experience and moral authority.

Possible Misconceptions

  • Some students appeared to conflate legal permissibility with moral permissibility. There was occasional confusion between arguments about what should be legally allowed versus what might be morally justified, suggesting students might benefit from clearer distinctions between ethical frameworks and policy considerations.
  • Several discussions revealed confusion about the distinct claims in Marquis's "future like ours" argument. Some students misunderstood this position as a traditional personhood argument rather than recognizing its focus on the deprivation of future experiences, indicating a potential need for clarification of philosophical positions.
